The Global Surveillance and Monitoring System

The WHO Global Surveillance and Monitoring System (GSMS) is a technical programme initiated in 2010 to address the global threat posed by substandard and falsified medical products (SFMP). These products undermine all investments in public health, compromise patient safety, and erode trust in health systems. The GSMS serves as WHO’s central instrument to prevent, detect, and respond to SFMP incidents worldwide.

A Three-Pillar Strategy

The holistic WHO strategy to combat SFMP is articulated on three pillars:

  • Prevent: Secure the supply of (and demand for) quality and safe medical products.
  • Detect: Increase surveillance and improve information sharing.
  • Respond: Prevent recurrence and strengthen regulatory systems.

GSMS Purpose and Objectives

To address any problem, it is necessary to first understand it. The GSMS was established to determine the scope, scale, and socio-economic harm caused by SF medical products. Its overall objectives are to:

  • Improve the quantity, quality, and analysis of data on SFMP.
  • Assess the impact of SFMP on health systems and populations.
  • Identify at-risk products, vulnerable populations, and supply chain weaknesses.
  • Support Member States in developing policies and procedures.
  • Inform investment and capacity-building efforts to strengthen regulatory systems.

GSMS Services to countries

WHO Member States collaborate with GSMS through designated focal points, supplying data and receiving support in return. The GSMS database serves as the foundation on which a range of bespoke services can be provided to countries:

  1. Data Collection, Analysis, and Coordination: The GSMS collects and validates reports of SFMP incidents globally. These reports are securely stored and analysed to identify trends, inform risk assessments, and guide technical responses. The system also facilitates international coordination and information exchange among WHO member States.
  2. Risk Communication and Surveillance: The GSMS promotes structured and systematic reporting. WHO issues communicates on SFMP in different ways to support national regulatory authorities (NRA) to prevent-detect-respond to the issue : alerts, information notices for IVD users, calls to action (to help respond), watch lists (to help detect), and threat assessments (to help prevent). Support is also provided for risk-based post market surveillance activities, and implementing National Action Plans, guided by WHO normative and standard setting work.
  3. Capacity Building and Technical Assistance: WHO provides training, tools, and technical support to strengthen national regulatory systems. This includes online courses, workshops, and tailored assistance in incident management, data analysis, and crisis communication. The GSMS also supports the development of national surveillance strategies and operational procedures.

GSMS Approach

The GSMS collects data which is analyzed into actionable information, used by policy makers and operational stakeholders responsible for safeguarding the quality and safety of medical products available to patients.

The primary platform for data submission is the GSMS Portal, a secure online interface that allows authenticated users to:

  • Notify SFMP incidents to the WHO.
  • Access individual or organization-level repositories of reports.
  • Search the GSMS database.
  • Access training materials, guidelines, and contact directories, etc.

Submitted data is reviewed by the WHO technical team, who validates the report, liaises with the reporting person, inputs any available additional contextual or missing information, etc. New incidents are prioritized by the WHO technical team based on various criteria such as the need for technical assistance, hazard characterization, etc.

The case-reporting database uses standardised forms that allows for structured data input. It is important to note that, because of the case-reporting nature of the database, there can be no extrapolations to prevalence of SFMP by geographical area, product type, etc.

While the primary data sources are officially nominated focal points from national regulatory authorities, the GSMS also collects data from other authenticated and authorised contributors, such as NGOs, academia, and pharmaceutical companies, who report incidents of SF medicines and vaccines. Medical device manufacturers can also report incidents involving in vitro diagnostic devices.

The Member State Mechanism

The WHO Member State Mechanism (MS-mech) is an intergovernmental platform established by the World Health Assembly in 2012 (WHA65.19) to coordinate global action against substandard and falsified (SF) medical products. It is led by WHO Member States and supported by the WHO Secretariat, operating on principles of transparency, inclusiveness, and public health protection.

The objectives of the MS-mech are to:

  • protect public health and promote access to affordable, quality, efficacious and safe medical products,
  • promote the prevention and control of SF medical products and associated activities

Based on insights provided by the GSMS, and national expertise, the MS-mech prioritises activities, and WHO leverages evidence to translate these into policies and guidance to strengthen national capacity in various institutional fields (primarily regulation).

The MS-mech provides a formal forum for countries to share experiences, and best practices in preventing, detecting, and responding to SF medical products. The mechanism operates through biennial workplans endorsed by its Steering Committee and the World Health Assembly. Member States are encouraged to contribute actively and adapt MS-mech outputs to their national contexts. They participate through official representation in MS-mech meetings and working groups.

The MS-mech complements the technical work of the WHO Global Surveillance and Monitoring System (GSMS) by addressing the political and regulatory dimensions of SFMP control. It also helps align global efforts, inform policy, and mobilize resources.

By fostering collaboration and shared responsibility at a global policy level, the MS-mech plays a vital role in safeguarding public health and ensuring the integrity of medical product supply chains worldwide.

SFMP incidents can be recorded in the GSMS only after they have been detected locally, and then reported (i.e. information shared) to the WHO. As stated above, data in the GSMS cannot be extrapolated to any form of prevalence of SFMP – however it is indeed an indicator of the capacity to detect and report. The volume of incidents and products recorded in the GSMS database are usually aggregated by WHO regions. In this semi-automated analysis, we used OpenRouter API key and MyGPT function to:

  1. Assign an income-level group to each country in in the dataset: High income, Upper-middle income, Lower-middle income, Low income.
  2. Assign bespoke population based groupings to each country: very large (>100 million) , large (40-100 million) , medium (10-40 million), small (1-10 million), microstate (<1 million).

In the case of these two continuously changing indicators, an API key is useful. The enhanced dataset was then uploaded to Cursor, prompted to run an analysis, present the data by income groups and population size groups, instead of regional groups, and provide an interpretation of these relationships (using Pearson's or any other relevant correlation coefficient).

The GSMS database is relational database, with several interconnected entities. In particular the “Incident” and “Product” entities have a hierarchical parent-child relationship.

Incident: An incident record corresponds to a notification of one or more medical products (medicines or vaccines) that are either suspected or confirmed to be substandard or falsified. These products must have been discovered at the same place and time, or as part of the same activity. Each incident is treated as a unique event and is recorded as a parent entity in the GSMS database.

Product: A product record is a child entity linked to an incident. It represents a specific medicine or vaccine reported within that incident. Each product record is uniquely identified by a combination of product name, batch/lot number, expiry date, and manufacturing date. If the same product is reported through different incidents, separate product records are created for each occurrence.

These definitions are foundational to the GSMS database structure, enabling differentiation between the context of discovery (incident) and the specific items involved (products), which supports more precise analysis of the scale and distribution of SFMP.

Register as a Focal Point

GSMS focal points are officially nominated by the national regulatory authorities (or ministry of health) from their country and serve as the primary national liaison with WHO on issues related to SFMP. Their roles and responsibilities are covered by terms of reference and include:

  • Notification of SFMP incidents.
  • Coordination of multilateral responses.
  • Participation in training and use of GSMS tools.
  • Ensuring confidentiality and secure information sharing.
  • Support to implement WHO normative guidance on regulatory system strengthening.

The focal point network enables the GSMS to function as a truly global system. It ensures that data flows efficiently from the local to the international level, allowing WHO to detect trends, issue alerts, and coordinate responses across borders. The network also promotes peer learning and collaboration, as focal points share experiences and best practices through regional and global forums.

By empowering focal points with the tools, training, and authority to act, WHO strengthens national regulatory systems and enhances global health security. The effectiveness of the GSMS depends heavily on the engagement and performance of this network, making it a vital component of the international effort to combat substandard and falsified medical products.
